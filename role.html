Core Weekly Duties

Every TA position is a little different depending on the course, but most UG TAs can expect the following as their standard responsibilities each week.

Required
Hosting a Lab or Discussion Section

Most TAs are assigned to at least one lab or discussion section per week. This means showing up on time, helping students work through the material, answering questions, and occasionally delivering a brief walkthrough or introduction. Before each session, do the lab or assignment yourself, understanding it from the student's perspective makes a real difference. Creating slides or an outline for your section is common and is payable time.

Required
Office Hours - Minimum 2 Hours per Week

TAs are typically expected to hold at least 2 hours of office hours per week. These can be in person, on Zoom, or hybrid depending on what the instructor allows. See the Office Hours page for details on format and expectations.

Required
Course Staff Meetings

Most courses hold weekly or biweekly meetings for TAs and the instructor. These keep everyone aligned on grading standards, upcoming assignments, and anything else going on with the course. Attendance is expected, and the time is payable.

Required
Student Communication

Responding to student questions through the course's preferred platform is part of the job. Check regularly throughout the week, especially around deadlines. Time spent on this is payable. See the Communication section below for more on which platforms are typically used.

Grading

Grading varies a lot depending on the course. In some cases it is required; in others it is optional but encouraged. Always confirm with your instructor at the start of the semester what is expected of you.

For example, in CSCI 1933, grading projects is a required TA duty, while grading quizzes is not required but is encouraged and compensated if you choose to do it. Your course will have its own setup.

Grading can involve judgment calls where TAs interpret a rubric differently. That is normal. Grading sessions are often held together with other TAs and lead TAs so you can ask questions and calibrate before grading on your own. When you hit an edge case, check with the lead TA or instructor rather than making the call solo.

Tip: Try to spread grading out over the time you are given rather than doing it all at once. Feedback quality tends to drop when you are grinding through a large batch in one sitting.

Labs, Discussion, and Review Sessions

Depending on your course, you may be running lab sections, leading discussion, or helping facilitate review sessions before exams. These typically involve:

  • Doing the lab or assignment yourself beforehand so you know what students are working through
  • Creating or reviewing slides, some courses provide them, others expect TAs to make their own. Both prep and creation time are payable.
  • Facilitating the session, keeping students on track, answering questions, and walking through concepts as needed
  • Staying a bit after the session to help students who are still working (This time is payable)
  • Running pre-exam review sessions if asked or if you offer, prep time and the session itself are both compensated

Proctoring Exams

Proctoring is not a required TA duty, but it is an opportunity to pick up additional paid hours if you are interested. When instructors ask for volunteers to proctor, it is a straightforward way to contribute more and get compensated for it. If you do proctor, you will typically help distribute and collect exams and monitor the room. All proctoring time is payable.

Communication Platforms

Each course has its own preferred communication channel. Make sure you know which platform your course uses and check it regularly. Common options include:

Discord Piazza Slack Canvas Email

Email is always an option, but be thoughtful about what personal information you share. You are a TA. Keep communication through official channels and avoid sharing personal contact details like your personal phone number or social media. That boundary protects both you and the students you are working with.
