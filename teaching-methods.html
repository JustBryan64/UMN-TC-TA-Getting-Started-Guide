The Goal

Your job as a TA is not to give students the answer. It is to help them find it. The most valuable thing you can do is build their ability to work through problems independently, not just fix their code or solve their homework for them.

Point them in the right direction and offer feedback for specific questions or errors. Allow them to make mistakes and work through those mistakes on their own. That process is where the actual learning happens.

Techniques for Helping Students

Technique
Ask Them to Walk You Through It

Before you look at the code or the problem yourself, ask the student to explain what they were trying to do and how they approached it. Asking them to walk you through their thought process reveals a lot about how they specifically wanted to solve the coding task and gives you a much better starting point than scanning their screen cold.

Technique
Think Out Loud

Walk through your own thought process out loud as you approach a problem together. "First I'm going to check what type this variable is..." Modeling your reasoning helps students understand not just the solution, but how to think about the problem.

Technique
Break It Down

If a student is stuck on something complex, help them decompose it into smaller pieces. Ask what the first thing that needs to happen is, and work forward one step at a time. This is especially useful during debugging sessions where the problem feels overwhelming.

Technique
Have Them Explain It Back

After walking a student through something, ask them to explain it back to you in their own words. This shows you quickly whether they actually understood or just nodded along, and it reinforces the learning either way.

Technique
Use Analogies

Abstract concepts land better when they are connected to something familiar. Pointers and memory addresses? A street address pointing to a house. Recursion? Russian nesting dolls. Find analogies that actually make sense for the concept you are explaining.

Debugging with Students

Debugging sessions are one of the best teaching opportunities you will have. Resist the urge to just spot the bug, use it as a chance to teach a systematic approach.

  1. Read the error message together. A lot of students skip this entirely. Train them to start here every time.
  2. Reproduce the issue. Can they run it and see the error? Make sure the environment is actually set up correctly first.
  3. Isolate the problem. Where does the actual behavior first diverge from what was expected?
  4. Form a hypothesis. Ask what they think is causing it before anyone looks at the code.
  5. Test the hypothesis. Add a print statement, inspect a variable, or trace through the logic line by line.
Useful prompts during debugging: "What do you expect this line to do?" | "What value do you think X holds at this point?" | "What would happen if you removed this condition?"

Running a Lab or Discussion Section

  • Do the work yourself first. Going into a lab without having done the assignment is one of the fastest ways to lose credibility. Even a quick pass through it beforehand puts you in a much better position to help.
  • Start with a brief framing. Tell students what the goal of the session is and what they should be able to do by the end. A minute of setup saves a lot of confusion later.
  • Move around actively. Do not stand at the front the whole time. Walk around, check in with people proactively, and do not wait for hands to go up.
  • Manage your time across students. If you spend 20 minutes with one person while others are stuck, that is a problem. Set a reasonable limit on individual help and offer to follow up at the end of the session if needed.
  • Address shared confusion publicly. If several students are stuck on the same thing, take a moment to address the whole group. "Looks like a few of you are running into the same issue, let me go over it quickly."
  • End with a quick recap. Summarize the key concepts and common mistakes from the session. Even two or three minutes of this at the end is worth it.

Do's and Don'ts

Do
  • Let students work through the struggle before jumping in
  • Use their actual code as the teaching example
  • Acknowledge effort, not just correct answers
  • Be patient when the same concept keeps coming up
  • Check for understanding before moving on
Don't
  • Type on a student's keyboard for them
  • Give the answer just because it is faster
  • Make students feel bad for not knowing something
  • Skip over a question because it seems too basic
  • Dump too much information on them at once
