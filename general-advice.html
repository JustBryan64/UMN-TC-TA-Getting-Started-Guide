Approaching the Role

Being a TA is more than answering questions. It is a professional position that requires reliability, patience, and a genuine interest in helping students succeed. You are in a position between the instructor's expectations and the student experience, that matters.

The most effective TAs are the ones who are approachable. Being kind, patient, and easy to talk to goes a long way in getting students to actually come to you for help rather than struggling alone.

Keep in mind: You were in their seat not too long ago. Your ability to remember what it felt like to not understand something yet is one of your most useful qualities as a TA.

Professional Boundaries

Moving from student to TA means adjusting some relationships, especially if you are TAing a course with former classmates or friends. A few things to keep in mind:

  • Be friendly, but be consistent. Apply the same standards to everyone regardless of who they are to you outside the course.
  • Do not talk about grades or assignment specifics with friends outside of official channels like office hours or email.
  • If you have a conflict of interest, like grading someone you are close with, let your supervisor know in advance.
  • Do not share course materials, exam content, or rubrics with students outside the course.
  • Keep all communication through official platforms. Avoid DMing students on personal social media.

Communication Standards

How you communicate sets the tone for your relationship with students and reflects on you professionally.

  • Response time: Aim to reply within 24 to 48 hours on weekdays. If you will be unavailable for an extended stretch, let students know.
  • Tone: Be warm and approachable, but stay professional. Avoid overly casual language in written communication.
  • Clarity: If a student asks a vague question, ask a clarifying question before giving an answer that might not address what they actually need.
  • Consistency: If you answer a question publicly on Piazza or Discord, make sure that answer applies equally to all students.
  • Escalation: When in doubt, whether about a grade dispute, an accommodation, or student behavior, bring it to the course instructor. You do not have to handle everything on your own.

Academic Integrity

As a TA, you are in a position to notice things instructors might miss. Here is how to handle it:

  • Know the course's collaboration policy before the semester starts so you can recognize when something seems off.
  • If you suspect cheating or unauthorized collaboration while grading, do not penalize the student yourself. Flag it to the course instructor with your notes and any evidence you have.
  • Document what you observed, timestamps, screenshots, side-by-side comparisons if applicable, and let the instructor take it from there.
  • Do not accuse or threaten a student directly. Keep your observations factual and report through the proper channels.
See also: The UMN Resources page links to the EOAA misconduct reporting form and the official CS&E TA Handbook, which covers policy in more detail.

Quick Tips

Prep before you show up

Read through the lab or assignment before you TA it. Being one step ahead makes a noticeable difference in how confident and useful you are in the session.

It is okay to not know

"I am not sure, let me look that up" is a perfectly good answer. Students respect honesty a lot more than a confident wrong answer. No single TA knows everything.

Log hours as you go

Do not wait until the end of the pay period to enter your time. Track as you go so you do not undercount or forget anything.

Check in with your instructor

A quick message to your course instructor every now and then keeps you aligned and shows that you are engaged. Ask if there is anything they need from you.

Stay calm with frustrated students

If a student is upset about a grade or confused about something, stay level. Redirect to the rubric or the material rather than engaging emotionally.

Teaching reinforces your own knowledge

Do not treat the harder parts of your course as something to avoid. Explaining things you are not fully solid on is one of the best ways to actually understand them.
