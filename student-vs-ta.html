Student First, TA Second

The most important thing to keep in mind as an undergraduate TA is that you are a student first. You were hired to support a course, to run labs, hold office hours, and help students learn. But never at the expense of your own education.

1
Your courses and academic performance
Passing your classes, completing your own assignments, and keeping up with your coursework
2
Your TA duties
Labs, office hours, grading, communication, these are important, but secondary to your own education

If your TA duties are consistently cutting into your study time, your exam prep, or your wellbeing, that is a signal to have a conversation with your supervisor, not to push through and hope it balances out.

Days Off Are Okay

Being a TA is a part-time job, and like any part-time job, there are legitimate reasons to be absent. Taking a day off is fine, especially when you are sick, in the middle of a heavy exam week, dealing with something personal, or just need to step back for your own wellbeing.

If you need to miss a session: Give as much notice as you can. Let your course instructor know, and post in the class communication channel so students are not left waiting. If another TA can cover for you, great, but communicate first.
If you are sick: Do not push through office hours or a lab when you are unwell. It is not fair to you, and it is not fair to the students. Rest, recover, and send a quick message to your instructor.

You are not expected to be available at all times. You are a person not a machine.

What You Were Hired to Do

The core of the TA role is straightforward: host labs or discussion sections and support students during office hours. Everything else, grading, communication, creating materials, is important but supplementary. Keep that in mind.

  • You were not hired to be on call around the clock.
  • You were not hired to respond to messages at midnight before a deadline.
  • You were not hired to take on work that has not been assigned or approved by your instructor.
  • You were hired to show up, be present, and genuinely help students during your scheduled time.

You do not need to be everywhere all the time to do a good job. However, that doesn't mean you are not allowed to respond to late messages or help out students if you are available.

The Transition from Student to TA

It can feel a little strange to go from sitting in a course to helping run one, especially if you are TAing alongside people you know or recently took the class with. A few things worth keeping in mind as you settle into the role:

  • You do not need to know everything. No TA knows everything about the course content. There will be questions you cannot answer, and that is completely fine. Look it up, ask a fellow TA, or bring it to the instructor.
  • You are still learning too. Even if you have taken the course before, it is worth revisiting what you are not fully solid on. Teaching something is one of the best ways to deepen your own understanding of it.
  • Apply the rules consistently. Be approachable with everyone, including friends, but hold everyone to the same standard. Fairness protects you and the students.
  • Your fellow TAs are a resource. Do not try to figure everything out on your own. The other TAs on your course have different strengths and experiences. Lean on each other.

Keeping the Balance

Burnout is real, and undergraduate TAs are not immune to it. Here are a few signs that the balance might be off and it is worth checking in with your supervisor:

  • You are regularly working more hours than your position allocates
  • TA responsibilities are causing you to miss class or fall behind on your own coursework
  • You are feeling resentful, exhausted, or checked out from the role
  • Students are reaching out at all hours and you feel obligated to respond right away

A good supervisor wants to know when something is not working. Open communication is better than silently stretching yourself too thin, for your sake, for the students, and for the course.
